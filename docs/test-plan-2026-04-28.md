# 2. System overview

## What is Enki?

Enki is a **multi-tenant drilling-survey data platform**. It's
replacing Athena, an older application that stores measurements taken
inside oil and gas wells while they're being drilled.

The "survey" in *drilling survey* doesn't mean a questionnaire — it
means a measurement of the wellbore's position underground. Drillers
need to know exactly where the bottom of the hole is so they can hit
the geological target without colliding with neighbouring wells. Enki
stores those measurements and computes the trajectory of every well
from them.

## Tenant / Job / Well / Survey hierarchy

The data is organised as a strict tree:

```
Tenant ── (a customer organisation, e.g. PERMIAN)
   └── Job ── (a single drilling project, e.g. "Crest-North-Pad")
         └── Well ── (a physical wellbore, e.g. "Crest North 1H")
               ├── Tie-on ── (the anchor / starting point)
               ├── Surveys ── (the measurements down the wellbore)
               ├── Tubulars ── (casing, tubing, liner segments)
               ├── Formations ── (geological layers cut by the well)
               ├── Common measures ── (operational measurements)
               └── Magnetic reference ── (corrections for the local field)
```

This hierarchy maps directly to the URLs you'll be navigating, e.g.:

```
/tenants/PERMIAN/jobs/{guid}/wells/{int}/surveys
```

## What "multi-tenant" means

Each tenant gets its own pair of databases (Active + Archive) on SQL
Server. A user who's a member of `PERMIAN` only sees `PERMIAN` data.
Users with the system-wide `enki-admin` role (you and Mike) can see
all tenants. Tenant isolation is one of the things you'll verify.

# 3. Drilling-survey domain primer

This is the section to read before you start clicking. Every term
here will appear in the tests. If something doesn't make sense, ask;
the geometry takes a few minutes to click but it's not complicated.

## Measured depth (MD) and true vertical depth (TVD)

The **measured depth** of a station is how much pipe the driller has
fed into the hole to reach that point. It's the *length along the
wellbore*, not the depth straight down.

The **true vertical depth** is how far below the surface the station
*actually is*, measured straight down.

For a vertical well, MD = TVD. For a horizontal well, MD can be much
larger than TVD: a Wytch Farm ERD well goes ~1.6 km TVD but ~10.7 km
MD because most of the pipe is travelling sideways through the
reservoir.

```
       Surface
          │
          │← still vertical, MD == TVD
          │
          ╲
           ╲ ← building angle, MD > TVD
            ╲
             ╲___________________________
                                          │ ← horizontal,
                                          │   TVD constant,
                                          │   MD keeps growing
```

## Inclination and Azimuth

These two angles describe **which way the wellbore is pointing** at a
given station.

- **Inclination** — angle between the wellbore axis and straight
  down (vertical). Range 0–180°.
  - 0° = pointing straight down (vertical hole)
  - 90° = pointing horizontally (lateral section)
  - 180° = pointing straight up (rare, but the math allows it)
- **Azimuth** — compass direction the wellbore is heading, measured
  clockwise from true north. Range 0–360°.
  - 0° / 360° = north
  - 90° = east
  - 180° = south
  - 270° = west

Together, (inclination, azimuth) define a **unit tangent vector** —
the direction the wellbore is travelling at that station. The
trajectory math (next section) uses these tangents to figure out
where each station ends up in 3D space.

## Tie-on (the anchor station)

A wellbore measurement is always *relative* to a starting point. You
can't compute "where is this station in 3D" without knowing where the
station before it was. The anchor at the top of the chain is called
the **tie-on**.

A tie-on carries:
- Depth, inclination, azimuth (where the anchor sits)
- Northing, Easting (the surface coordinates of the anchor on the
  earth's grid — large numbers, like UTM coordinates)
- Vertical reference (the vertical depth assigned to the anchor)
- Sub-sea reference (depth below mean sea level)
- Vertical-section direction (the azimuth used for the V-sect
  projection — explained below)

In Enki, **every well has exactly one tie-on**. When you create a new
well, the system automatically creates a tie-on at all-zero values;
you fill it in when you know the surface location and reference
depths. If you click "Delete tie-on", the row is **reset to zero**
rather than removed — every well must keep an anchor on file or the
trajectory math has nothing to project against.

## The minimum-curvature method (the math, conceptually)

To compute *where* each station is in 3D space, Enki uses a standard
industry method called **minimum-curvature**. You don't need to
implement it; you just need to recognise what it does.

Given two consecutive stations and their tangent vectors, the
minimum-curvature method assumes the wellbore between them is a
**circular arc** — the smoothest, least-bendy path that smoothly
joins the two tangents. From that arc it computes:

- The change in northing, easting, and TVD between the two stations
- A measure of how sharply the wellbore is curving (DLS, below)
- A few other derived values (build rate, turn rate, vertical
  section)

The result: starting from the tie-on, every station gets a 3D
coordinate. Plot those coordinates and you have the well's
trajectory.

In Enki, the calculation is owned by **Marduk** — the math library
sitting under the WebApi. You shouldn't see Marduk in the UI directly,
but every survey edit triggers a recalc and the results land in the
grid columns labelled TVD, North, East, DLS, V-sect, Northing,
Easting, Build, and Turn.

## Computed columns explained

When you look at the Surveys grid, you'll see input columns and
computed columns. Here's what each computed column is:

- **TVD** (true vertical depth) — straight-down depth from the
  surface to the station.
- **Sub-sea** — depth below mean sea level (TVD adjusted for the
  surface elevation).
- **North** — northing displacement from the *tie-on*. Positive =
  north of the tie-on, negative = south of the tie-on. Small
  numbers (tens to thousands of metres / feet).
- **East** — easting displacement from the tie-on. Positive = east.
  Small numbers.
- **Northing** — absolute earth-grid northing (= tie-on Northing +
  North). Large numbers (millions of feet on the Texas state-plane,
  for instance).
- **Easting** — absolute earth-grid easting (= tie-on Easting +
  East).
- **DLS** (dogleg severity) — how sharply the wellbore is curving,
  in degrees per 30 m (or per 100 ft) of measured depth. A vertical
  hole has DLS ≈ 0. A typical build section has DLS around 1–3.
  Anything above 5 is steep; above 10 is a "hard dogleg" that can
  damage tools.
- **V-sect** (vertical section) — distance from the tie-on projected
  along a chosen compass direction (the *vertical-section direction*
  on the tie-on). Used for plotting "how far have I drilled in the
  target direction, ignoring the perpendicular drift". Always a
  one-dimensional number; positive = along the chosen direction,
  negative = opposite.
- **Build** — rate at which inclination is changing (degrees per 30 m
  / 100 ft).
- **Turn** — rate at which azimuth is changing (degrees per 30 m /
  100 ft).

## Magnetic reference

Magnetic-survey tools measure azimuth against the earth's magnetic
field, but the magnetic field doesn't point at true north — it
points at *magnetic* north, which drifts year to year. Each well
gets a **magnetic reference** triple recording the local correction
factors at the time the surveys were taken:

- **Declination** — angle between magnetic north and true north
  (degrees, east of true is positive).
- **Dip** — angle between the field vector and horizontal
  (degrees down from horizontal).
- **Total field** — magnitude of the local field in nanoteslas (nT).
  Typical land values are 45 000–55 000 nT.

For testing purposes you don't need to know the exact values — just
that the magnetic reference is per-well, can be edited, and can be
"cleared" (resetting the well's magnetics back to "not set").

## Tubulars, formations, common measures

Each well also carries operational data that isn't trajectory-related
but is part of the survey record:

- **Tubulars** — the metal pipe segments inside the wellbore. Casing
  (large pipe cemented to the formation), tubing (the production
  pipe), liner (a casing that hangs off another casing), drill
  string, etc. Each tubular has a from-MD, to-MD, diameter, and
  weight.
- **Formations** — the geological layers the well passes through.
  Each has a name (e.g. "Wolfcamp", "Sherwood", "Bone Spring"), a
  from-TVD, to-TVD, and a "resistance" value (a stand-in for any
  per-formation property the user wants to track).
- **Common measures** — operational measurements indexed by vertical
  depth range. Catch-all for operator-tracked values that don't fit
  the above.

These are simple list/edit pages; the tests for them are
straightforward CRUD checks.

## Anti-collision (travelling cylinder)

In a producing field there are often dozens of wells in close
proximity. While drilling a new well you must not collide with
existing ones — at the worst case, two wellbores intersecting can
release a high-pressure fluid stream that destroys both wells (this
is what happened in the **Macondo blowout** in 2010, and the
"Macondo-style relief-well intercept" demo in PERMIAN models exactly
the response).

The **anti-collision scan** projects every offset well's trajectory
relative to a target well and reports the closest-approach distance
at every depth. The most common visualisation is the **travelling
cylinder** — imagine a cylinder around the target's wellbore with
radius equal to the closest-approach distance to a given offset; as
you walk down the target wellbore, the cylinder shrinks where the
offset gets close and expands where it pulls away.

The most operationally critical anti-collision scenario is **SAGD**
(Steam-Assisted Gravity Drainage) — see the Cold Lake demo in BOREAL.
SAGD drills a producer-injector pair where the injector is held
~5 m directly above the producer over hundreds of metres of lateral.
This is the canonical SDI MagTraC ranging-tool scenario.

## Unit systems

Drilling spans both metric and US customary measurements depending on
the operator. Enki supports three:

- **SI** — strict metric: metres, degrees. This is the on-the-wire
  default (the database stores everything in SI).
- **Field** — US oilfield: feet, degrees. The Permian Basin uses
  this.
- **Metric** — operational metric: metres, degrees. UK / Norway /
  Canada use this.

The unit system is set per **Job**, so a tenant can have Field jobs
and Metric jobs side by side. The UI converts on display only — the
database is always SI.

You'll see this matter in tests where:
- The Surveys grid for a PERMIAN job shows depths in **ft**
- The Surveys grid for a NORTHSEA or BOREAL job shows depths in **m**
- Your *user preference* in Account Settings is the fallback when
  the page isn't job-scoped (e.g. anti-collision view across
  tenants).

# 4. Architecture & deployment

Enki runs as **three separate processes** in the dev rig. You don't
need to interact with them directly, but seeing what's running in
which window helps when something fails.

| Process | Default port | What it does |
| ------- | ------------ | ------------ |
| **Identity** | 5196 | OpenIddict OIDC server. Handles sign-in, sign-out, password reset, the user roster. |
| **WebApi**   | 5107 | The REST API. Owns the per-tenant database connections. Every UI page fetches data from here. |
| **Blazor**   | 5073 | The user interface. **This is the URL you put in the browser.** |

When you run `scripts/start-dev.ps1 -Reset`, three PowerShell windows
open in dependency order:

1. **Identity** comes up first — it has its own database (Enki_Identity)
   and seeds 11 users.
2. **WebApi** comes up second — applies the master-database migration
   and runs the seeders. This is where the demo tenants are
   provisioned and where Marduk's auto-calc fires for every seeded
   survey row.
3. **Blazor** comes up last — it waits for the other two to be
   reachable, then serves the UI.

If any one of them dies during testing, the others keep running and
the UI surfaces the failure as an error banner rather than crashing
the page.

# 19. Unit cascade — display + round-trip across every input

## What you're testing

Every numeric value in Enki has a **canonical SI form** stored in the
database, and a **display form** that the UI converts to before
showing the user. Length is the obvious example: 1500 metres in the
DB might appear as `1500 m` on a Metric job or `4921.26 ft` on a
Field job. The conversion fires both ways — when reading (SI → display)
and when typing (display → SI on save).

A bug in this layer is **silent**: the form looks like it works, the
chart draws something, the value doesn't crash anything — it's just
quietly the wrong number. Today's "type 100 m, save 0" bug was a
units-related binding failure that survived the existing check
because the old SUR-05 only verified the column header units, not
the actual numbers round-tripping through a save.

This section systematically verifies:

1. **The right unit is shown** for every quantity per system
   (reference card below).
2. **The cascade source** is correct — a Job-scoped page reads from
   the Job's `UnitSystem`; a non-Job-scoped page reads from your
   user preference.
3. **Round-trip integrity** — type X in display units, save, reload
   the same form, see X back. Done on both Field and Metric so a
   one-way conversion bug is caught regardless of direction.
4. **Override units** — Tubular Diameter is in `in` on Field and
   `mm` on Metric, regardless of the cascade.

## What "round-trip broken" looks like

If a round-trip test fails, the failure mode tells you which side
of the conversion is broken:

- **Saved value is 0** when you typed a number → the form's
  display→SI binding never fired (the bug from today's commit
  `c79164b` — render-mode missing on the form).
- **Saved value is the right magnitude in the wrong unit** (e.g.
  type `5000 ft`, reload, see `5000 m` or `1524 ft`) → one of
  `UnitConverter.ToSi` / `FromSi` is using the wrong unit enum.
- **Saved value is a slight float-precision difference** (e.g.
  `5000.000003`) → expected and harmless; double-precision round-
  trips are not bit-exact.
- **Column suffix is wrong** (e.g. `Depth (m)` on a Field job) →
  cascade source bug; the page isn't reading from the Job's
  `UnitSystem`.
- **Override didn't fire** (e.g. Tubular Diameter shows `ft` on
  Field instead of `in`) → `OverrideUnit` parameter not being
  passed to the `<UnitInput>` on that form.

## Optimistic concurrency

### What you're testing

Every mutation endpoint (PUT / PATCH / lifecycle) now carries a
**RowVersion** — a token the client sends back with every save that
proves "the record I'm about to change is the same one I loaded".
The server enforces this at the SQL UPDATE: if the row's
`rowversion` column has moved since the client read it, the
database matches zero rows, EF raises a concurrency exception, and
the API returns **409 Conflict** with a reload-and-retry message.

In practice you'll see this when two users (or two browser tabs)
edit the same record at the same time. One save wins; the other
gets a red alert banner reading

> **Conflict — The {entity} was modified by another user since you
> loaded it. Reload to see the latest values, then re-apply your
> edit.**

The wire pattern is identical across nine entities (Survey, Tie-on,
Tubular, Formation, Common Measure, Magnetics, Well, Job, Tenant)
so the tests below exercise a representative slice rather than
every endpoint individually. **Tenant Members is intentionally out
of scope** — see CONC-70 below.

A drilling-domain nuance worth knowing before you start: editing
a Survey or a Tie-on triggers Marduk's **auto-recalc**, which
recomputes every other survey row on the same well and bumps each
one's rowversion as a side-effect. So if user A edits Survey #5
and user B is concurrently editing Survey #6 *on the same well*,
user B's save will 409 even though the two users picked different
rows. That's deliberate — user B's view of the trajectory has been
invalidated by user A's edit, every downstream computed column
(TVD, North, East, DLS, …) is stale, and the reload-and-retry
prompt is the right answer. CONC-30 / CONC-31 below verify this
trade-off fires the way the design says it will.

# 29. Glossary

Terms used in this document, in alphabetical order. If you encounter
something that isn't here, ask Mike to add it for the next pass.

**Active database** — the per-tenant database holding live data.
Each tenant has an Active and an Archive database; archived jobs
move from Active to Archive (a future feature; not in this build).

**Anti-collision** — the practice of ensuring a new well being
drilled doesn't intersect any existing wells. Implemented via the
travelling-cylinder scan.

**Audit columns** — `CreatedAt`, `CreatedBy`, `UpdatedAt`,
`UpdatedBy` on every entity. Tracks who changed what, when.

**Auto-calc** — the automatic recalculation of a well's trajectory
that fires after every Survey or Tie-on mutation. Driven by Marduk's
`ISurveyCalculator.Process`.

**Azimuth** — the compass direction the wellbore is heading,
clockwise from true north, in degrees. 0 / 360 = north, 90 = east,
180 = south, 270 = west.

**Blazor** — the UI host (port 5073). Server-side rendered
ASP.NET Core 10 app.

**Build (rate)** — how fast inclination is changing per unit of
measured depth, in degrees per 30 m (or per 100 ft).

**Bootstrap tenant** — the first tenant the master seeder
provisions on `start-dev.ps1 -Reset`. PERMIAN.

**Casing** — large-diameter steel pipe cemented to the formation
to keep the wellbore stable. A Tubular type.

**Common measure** — operational measurement on a well, indexed
by a vertical depth range. Catch-all for operator-tracked values.

**Declination** — the angle between magnetic north and true north
at a given location, in degrees east of true.

**Dip** — angle of the local magnetic field below horizontal, in
degrees.

**Dogleg** — a section of wellbore where the trajectory is
curving. A "hard dogleg" is a steeply curving section.

**DLS** (Dogleg Severity) — measure of how sharply the wellbore is
curving, in degrees per 30 m (or per 100 ft) of measured depth.

**Easting** — east displacement on the earth's grid (e.g. UTM).
Large absolute value; the small-scale displacement from the tie-on
is **East**.

**ERD** (Extended Reach Drilling) — wells with very long horizontal
sections relative to TVD. Wytch Farm M-series in NORTHSEA is the
demo.

**Field (unit system)** — US oilfield: feet for depth, degrees for
angles. PERMIAN.

**Formation** — a geological layer the well passes through.

**Identity** — the OIDC server (port 5196). OpenIddict + ASP.NET
Identity.

**Inclination** — angle between the wellbore axis and straight
down (vertical), in degrees. 0 = vertical, 90 = horizontal.

**Job** — a single drilling project under a tenant. Contains
wells.

**Kick-off** — the section of a well where it transitions from
vertical to inclined. The build curve.

**LAS** (Log ASCII Standard) — a formal text format for log /
survey data. Sections begin with `~V`, `~W`, `~C`, `~P`, `~O`, `~A`.

**Lateral** — a section of well drilled near-horizontally, usually
through the producing formation.

**Macondo** — the 2010 Gulf of Mexico blowout that triggered the
canonical relief-well intercept response. Modelled by PERMIAN's
`MC252-Relief` job.

**Marduk** — the math library that computes trajectories.
Standalone repo; integrated into Enki as a project reference.

**Master database** — the single Enki_Master database holding the
tenant registry, user templates, and system settings. Per-tenant
databases are separate.

**MD** (Measured Depth) — distance along the wellbore from
surface, in metres or feet. Always increases as the bit moves
deeper.

**Metric (unit system)** — operational metric: metres for depth,
degrees for angles. NORTHSEA, BOREAL.

**Minimum-curvature** — the standard industry method for computing
trajectory between two survey stations. Treats the wellbore as a
circular arc joining two unit tangents.

**Multi-tenant** — design where each customer organisation gets
its own database pair. Tenants are isolated from each other.

**nT** (nanotesla) — unit of magnetic flux density. Earth's field
is around 25 000–65 000 nT depending on latitude.

**North** — north displacement *from the tie-on* (small scale).
The earth-grid northing is **Northing** (large absolute value).

**OIDC** (OpenID Connect) — the auth protocol Identity uses.

**Optimistic concurrency** — the design pattern where two clients
can both load a record at the same time, but only the *first* save
wins; the second client gets a 409 and is asked to reload. Enforced
in Enki via a `RowVersion` token round-tripped between the API and
the UI on every mutation. The alternative (pessimistic locking)
would hold a database lock for the duration of the user's edit
session — unworkable for a web UI where the "session" is whatever
time elapses between the page load and the save click.

**Pad** — a single surface site where multiple wells are drilled.
PERMIAN's 8-well pad has 8 wells whose surface points are within
~10 m of each other.

**Plan view** — the looking-down view of a wellbore trajectory
(X = East, Y = North).

**Reservoir cell** — a sub-region of a producing formation
targeted by a single horizontal well.

**RowVersion** — an 8-byte token (SQL Server `rowversion` column,
auto-incremented on every UPDATE) that the server uses to detect
concurrent edits. Wire format is base64 (12 ASCII characters). Sent
from server → client on every read; sent from client → server on
every save; the server's UPDATE includes a `WHERE rowversion = @v`
predicate so a stale token matches zero rows and the database
declines the write.

**SAGD** (Steam-Assisted Gravity Drainage) — a heavy-oil recovery
technique where a steam injector and an oil producer are drilled
~5 m apart over a long horizontal section. BOREAL demo.

**Sub-sea** — TVD adjusted for the surface elevation; depth below
mean sea level.

**Surface** — the top of the wellbore at ground/sea level. The
tie-on is typically at or near surface.

**TD** (Total Depth) — the deepest point of the wellbore.

**Tenant** — a customer organisation. Each has its own pair of
databases.

**Tie-on** — the anchor station that the trajectory math
projects from. Every well has at least one (auto-created at zero
on Well creation).

**Trajectory** — the 3D path of a wellbore in space, computed
from the survey stations and the tie-on.

**Travelling cylinder** — anti-collision visualisation showing
the closest-approach distance between a target wellbore and each
offset wellbore at every depth.

**Tubular** — a section of metal pipe inside the wellbore.
Includes Casing, Tubing, Liner, drill string, etc.

**Turn (rate)** — how fast azimuth is changing per unit of
measured depth, in degrees per 30 m (or per 100 ft).

**TVD** (True Vertical Depth) — straight-down depth from surface
to a station. For a vertical well, MD = TVD; for a horizontal
well, MD ≫ TVD.

**Unit system** — SI / Field / Metric. Set per Job; cascades to
all of the Job's wells.

**V-sect** (Vertical Section) — distance from the tie-on
projected along a chosen compass direction. One-dimensional.

**VSD** (Vertical Section Direction) — the compass direction
used for the V-sect projection. Stored on the tie-on.

**WebApi** — the REST API host (port 5107). Owns the per-tenant
database connections and runs Marduk's auto-calc.

**Wellbore** — the physical hole in the ground that surveys
describe. Synonymous with "well" in common usage.

**WMM** (World Magnetic Model) — the global model for magnetic
declination / dip / total field. Updated every 5 years; current
revision is WMM-2025 (rolling forward to WMM-2030 in 2025).
